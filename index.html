1. Introduccion

Rick and Morty is an adult animated science fiction sitcom created by producers Justin Roiland and Dan Harmon and originally broadcasted in the webpage Adult Swim1 . It narrates the intergalactic misadventures of Morty and his alcoholic, scientist grandfather, Rick. Even though they are the two main characters, other members of the family appear, such as Summer, Morty’s sister, and Morty’s parents, Beth and Jerry Smith.

The series has its origins in an animated short parody of Back to the Future2 created by Roiland for film festival Channel 1013 in 2006 called The Real Animated Adventures of Doc and Mharti. After contacts between Adult Swim, Roiland and Harmon several years later, Rick and Morty premiered on December 2, 2013, and due to its success, its third season is going to be released in late 2016.

Up until now, one may ask, what can make this sitcom different from others, or worth analyzing? As said above, in its origin the Doc and Mharti short was created as a parody to Back to the Future. This is clearly shown in the many resemblances the series has, and I could comment on the similarities and differences that are present; or in the way its characteristic humour may remind to that of other animated series like The Simpsons (in his early episodes), Futurama, Family Guy or even South Park. However this will not be the case.

What I am going to do in this essay, instead, is a critical analysis of today’s society, in order to see, through the series, what the effects of technological advances are and the problems they bring; what society’s beliefs in political and religious terms are; and what is done to solve (or evade) the consequences of modern life. For this purpose, I will focus on how these aspects are reflected throughout its two seasons by paying especial attention to two terms, nihilism4and escapism5

2. The consequences of technology upon humanity

The development (sometimes overdevelopment) of technologies has led to the creation of the modern society, a place in which a life without electronical devices is hard to imagine. As a way to defend them, most say that their benefits are huge, e.g. Hertlein & Ancheta (2014)6, but do these always bring good consequences?

Nowadays, it is almost impossible to see a person without a smartphone on his hands, robotics has advanced to the point that in Japan there are hotels that when doing the book in, a robot with a person-like appearance or even a dinosaur attends to you. We are so dependent, and obsessed, on machines that a question arises ‘where is this going to lead?’

To my mind come films such as I, Robot7 and Ex-Machina8 . In the first, robots are sold as a way to protect and help people, but they are so assimilated and so integrated in human’s everyday life that they realize that they are more intelligent and better than their creators, who are subject to death by, e.g. illnesses, so that they rebel and try to submit humanity to their control. On the latter a scientist is investigating about the perfect AI and proves it in women cyborgs. When he finds it, the robot’s consciousness is so evolved that she rebels and kills the scientist, escaping to the outside world. Thus, in these two cases, there is a direct critique on the results brought about by the power, importance and overdependence technologies are given nowadays.

Extrapolating this to Rick and Morty (R&M from now on), the second chapter of season one called “Lawnmowner Dog” can be taken as an example. Rick, by petition of Jerry, creates a device in order to make Snuffles, Morty’s dog, smarter. As it is said in “Rick and Morty: A Sociological Analysis” (2015)9:

Originally the increased intelligence of Snuffles is used for the family’s entertainment. Snuffles quickly becomes dissatisfied with the status quo of humans ruling over dogs

The comparison can be made to other modern technologies such as smart phones, television and nuclear weapons. Debord (1967, p.7) describes how these innovations empower humans but isolate us from each other. While originally invented to empower and free humans, arguments can be made they have enslaved and isolated us in various ways.

And this is in fact what is happening, there is a reversal in the dichotomy10 ‘human-robot’. Humans are the ones thought as having feelings for others, while robots do not. However, as we are more and more plugged to machines, we are no more feeling empathy for other human beings, whereas the robots we create can be said to be more rational. This situation makes robots question why humankind must be on top, with their later rebellion (as in the examples above), and it also makes people selfishly blind, not caring about other members of the species, which leads to different reactions.

Taking as an example the character of Rick, in the first episode11 of R&M, he wants to destroy humanity, as he thinks that it has lived its moment and it must all start again, and wants his grandson to repopulate the world. At the end, after considering the consequences, he changes his mind. On another occasion, this time in the sixth episode of season one12, Rick is burying his own body of other dimension in the garden, showing no emotion for having killed one Rick, one human being. Morty, on the other hand, is struggling to do the same with his self, thinking on the effects of a lost life. “This is a realization that humanity itself is flawed and ultimately will care not for its own existence”13.
